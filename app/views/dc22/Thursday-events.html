The Office for National Statistics (ONS) is the largest producer of official statistics in the UK and handles a huge quantity of data covering everything from the economy to health to population and Census statistics. 11:00am to 12:00pm The empty maps of Wales

Welsh is an official language in the UK. Public bodies that serve the people of Wales are required to provide services in Welsh and English. Public bodies cannot, currently, provide digital mapping services in Welsh. It is not possible, for example, to obtain Welsh-language tiles from the Ordnance Survey. Official sources of Welsh names for places and features are far from comprehensive and using alternative sources can be complex in technical and policy terms.

Mapio Cymru is a project hosted by Data Orchard CIC that has aimed to highlight and solve the problem of the empty maps. The project provides a full set of mapping tiles covering Wales using only Welsh-language data (from OpenStreetMap and Wikidata) and works with public bodies to help them solve the problem of empty maps.

In this presentation Mapio Cymru will talk about the problem, why it matters, what has been done to address it so far and what more public bodies could do to address it in the future.

Format: Presentation
Session leads: Carl Morris, Ben Proctor and David Wyn Williams from Mapio Cymru

12:00pm to 1:00pm Automated SIC & SOC Matching: the Classification Index Matching Service

In this session, we will be presenting the new ONS Classification Index Matching Service, which will eventually be available within the Integrated Data Service.

Fully understanding the UK economic and labour market landscape requires the classification and categorization of the many different occupations and economic activity areas for production of official statistics. This is achieved by matching survey and census responses to the UK Standard Industrial Classification of Economic Activities (SIC) and Standard Occupational Classification (SOC) frameworks. Unfortunately, existing matching tools only automate a small fraction of typical responses, and require large and expensive clerical teams to match the remainder. To make matters worse, there is currently no centralised standard for performing this matching, leading to inefficiencies and incompatibilities between data sets.

The Classifications team has been working hard to remedy this, producing an innovative, one-stop tool to be a core part of the Reference Data Management Framework and cross-government Integrated Data Service. Our journey has thoroughly investigated existing tools, testing their limitations and exploring new ideas. Join us as we show you how our findings led us to combined the best parts of deterministic and machine learning techniques to create a distributed, automated, world-leading hybrid tool ideally suited to the new data-rich digital world.

Format: Presentations plus Q&A
Session leads: Alex Howard, Lucy Fergusson and Suzanne Fry. Office for National Statistics.
Open to: All

12:30pm to 1:00pm Health Index: new value from existing data

The Health Index is a new statistic by ONS, enabling the public and policy-makers to track how the nation’s health is improving or declining over time. The Index summarises data from a variety of sources into a single value, making health trends easier to follow than ever before; but also provides access to the underlying components, so users can understand which aspects of health and its determinants are driving changes.
This presentation will outline how the ONS has made new use of existing data sources to expand the public health data landscape, and also describe how data producers can make their own data available in a way which enables re-use for products like the Health Index.

1:00pm to 1:45pm Asking the right questions: A conversation toolkit for administrative data

Do you use administrative data to produce statistics?

If so, join us to find out about a toolkit ONS researchers are developing that could help you.

The Office for Statistics Regulation state the importance of statistical producers communicating with their admin data supply partners to help understand the data they are using. It is advised that this communication should be on-going to ensure producers are aware of and consider any impacts of changes to datasets.

To support these vital conversations, we are developing a conversation toolkit which will consist of:

- a comprehensive question bank
- a conversation template
- guidance for communicating with supply partners

Join our show and tell session to find out more about the toolkit, to provide your feedback and to help inform its design.

The toolkit that they are developing seeks to support implementation of specific aspects of the guidance on Quality Assurance of Administrative Data, as set out by the Office for Statistics Regulation, and the Administrative Data Quality Framework which is currently being developed by their colleagues at ONS.
