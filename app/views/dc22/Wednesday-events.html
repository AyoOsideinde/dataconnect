9:00am to 9:45am FAIR data from principles to practice: How the UK public sector can use FAIR

Almost anyone who works with data has had problems with finding, accessing, using and reusing it. The FAIR data principles are a framework for managing and publishing data to unlock its value for the whole data ecosystem. They aim of the principles is to make data more Findable, Accessible, Interoperable and Reusable (FAIR). FAIR was created by academics to support the research community, and this session will explore the potential for their use in the UK public sector.

The session will be a short presentation on the principles, followed by an open discussion to identify current use in government, and whether there are opportunities to incorporate them further. If you are currently using the principles, or if you would like to find out more about how they might be suitable for you, please do come along and be ready to discuss!

Please note this session is interactive and attendees will be expected to participate.

Format: Presentation followed by open discussion
Speaker/s: Kathryn Reynolds, Shona Nicol, Smerah Akbar

10:30am to 11:00am Increasing the effective use of the data by the Gambling Commission

In this presentation the Gambling Commission will share its experiences as it undergoes a statistics improvement programme. The Commission is interested in why and how people gamble, how this differs across different segments of the population as well as how behaviour changes over time including those who experience harm from their gambling.

It also needs to understand the gambling opportunities available, how the market is evolving and what industry is doing. To do this the Commission gathers evidence from a range of data sources, and it is on a mission to improve the range and quality of the data it gathers.

The Commission will discuss the work it has been undertaking in one area to introduce a new Gambling Survey for Great Britain as well as its ambitions for the future including tracking behaviour changes over time through longitudinal research and exploring opportunities for data linkage.

We are really interested in hearing from people who may have experience of or are starting to link up about opportunities for data linkage.

The Gambling Commission is overseeing the development of the new Gambling Survey for Great Britain which will measure participation and the prevalence of problem gambling amongst adults in Great Britain.

11:00am to 11:45am Geoplace and Local Government Association - Mission 3: Transforming for a digital future: a roadmap for digital and data with land and property

Data management is the most important part of modern government and its most intractable limitation. Public services are only as good as the data that powers them. A digital future needs a consistent, joined up evidence base to underpin decision making.

Access to authoritative and dynamically updated information about people, land and property, businesses and assets is essential for a government that is connected and engaged with addressing the wellbeing and resilience of people and place right across the UK.

Reducing duplication, automating manual processes, joining together disparate datasets to reveal insights will save billions of pounds, foster innovation and enable public services to be transformed to achieve the right outcomes.

This session will discuss the need for open standards and ‘key registers’ for critical data assets such as land and property information to support a cross-cutting, functional model for government. It will outline the value of a consistent, joined up evidence base to underpin all decisions around land and property, and service delivery to people at places. After all, everything happens somewhere.

This session is aimed at Chief Data Officers and the people responsible for delivering the government’s 2022-25 Roadmap for Digital and Data.

11:00am to 12:00pm Effective Data Visualisation in the DWP – A Story

DWP MI is a strategic platform for delivering Management Information through data visualisations. DWP MI provides a common set of reporting services and solutions, which support decision making across the Lines of Business and enabling self-service.

This is specific to Ops MI:
The content within the Ops MI dashboard represents the four ‘Pillars of performance’ and is updated, every Tuesday on a weekly basis.
These pillars of performance support the two main outcomes of Universal Credit Full Service

  • 1. Paying claimants accurately and on time
  • 2. Helping people into work and working more

The four pillars of performance are:
  • • Payment Timeliness
  • • Labour Market activities
  • • Decision Making and Appeals
  • • Claimant Contact

Each of these pillars is made up of multiple ‘Levers’ and each lever measured has a proven correlation to a positive outcome for the claimant.

3:00pm to 3:30pm The world won’t let you RAP and what to do about it!

What can Back to the Future, physics and functional programming teach us about making Reproducible Analytical Pipelines?
In this talk Rick Moynihan and Robin Gower debunk the myths surrounding reproducibility, describe why so few data projects and processes ever seem to achieve it and provide data professionals with practical steps they can take to improve the reproducibility of their automation and data processing.

Robin Gower dreams of a day when data scientists can focus on analysis instead of spending their time preparing data! To this end, he works as a Data Designer at Swirrl helping publishers to express their datasets with linked-data. He draws on 15 years experience consulting as an economist and statistician for the UK public and social sectors.
Robin is a founding member of Open Data Manchester and wrote and maintains several open source software libraries.

Rick Moynihan is head of engineering at Swirrl and has been helping government clients reliably publish data for nearly a decade.
